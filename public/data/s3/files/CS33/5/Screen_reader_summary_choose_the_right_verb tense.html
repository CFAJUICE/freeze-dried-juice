Choose the right verb tense: Putting it all together

Present tense

Present tense describes an event in the present. Example: Each morning, I run to catch the train.

How to form the present tense

When the verb follows a singular noun or "he," "she," or "it," add an S or E S to the end of the verb. Example: She runs to catch the train.
If the action is happening while you're speaking, use "I am" or "I'm" with the I N G form of the verb. Example: I am running to catch the train right now.
When an event began in the past and continues into the present, use the verb "have" or "has." Example: I have learned a lesson about leaving on time.

Past tense

Past tense describes events taking place in the past. Example: She walked to the train station this morning.

How to form the past tense

For a single event in the past, Add D or E D to the end of the verb. Example: The train pulled away.
For two past events, one before the other, use the verb "had." Example: She had reached the train when it pulled away.
Caution! Do not use "had" when describing a single event in the past.
Note! Some verbs do not follow a pattern for forming the past tense. Check a dictionary if you are not sure.

Future tense

Future tense describes events taking place in the future. Example: The train will leave soon.

How to form the future tense

Use the word "will" before the verb. Example: We will be late and we will miss the train.

Showing order of events

Decide when events occurred and use the same tense.
If the events happened in the past, use the past tense. Example: He stubbed his toe and spilled coffee on himself.
If the events are happening in the present, use the present tense. Example: He stubs his toe and spills coffee on himself.
Shift tense only when the events happened at different times.
If the events started in the present and shifted to the past, start with the present tense and then shift to the past tense. Example: She is happy that she caught the train.
If the events started in the past and shifted to the future, start with the past tense and then shift to the future tense. Example: I almost missed the train and will leave earlier next time.
Remember! Think about when each event occurred and choose the correct tense.

Avoiding common errors

Lie versus Lay

"Lie" means to rest in a reclining position. The past tense of "lie" is "lay."
"Lay" means to put or place something. The past tense of "lay" is "laid."
Example: Yesterday a bear lay on my towel after I laid it down on the beach.

Choose versus Chose

"Choose" means to select one option from a group. The past tense of "choose" is "chose." Example: Today, I choose to stay inside, out of the sun. Yesterday, I chose to go to the beach without sunscreen.
