Which pronoun should I use?

Choosing the correct pronoun to replace a noun

You can use a pronoun to replace a noun in any part of a sentence, but you need to use the form of the pronoun that matches the noun it is replacing. Below are example sentences using different forms of nouns, and guiding questions to help you choose the correct pronouns to replace them with.

John uses John's computer for email.

Which pronoun should be used to replace the noun "John's?" To decide, ask yourself:

Is the noun the subject of the sentence, the object, or possessive? "John's" is a possessive noun. Next, ask yourself:

Is the noun singular or plural? "John's" is a singular noun. Finally, ask yourself:

Is the noun masculine, feminine, or neuter? "John's" is a masculine singular possessive noun, so you should replace it with the pronoun "his."

Now the sentence is, "John uses his computer for email."

Tracie and Alana worked all night. Tracie and Alana are tired

Which pronoun should be used to replace the noun "Tracie and Alana" in the sentence "Tracie and Alana are tired?" To decide, ask yourself:

Is the noun the subject of the sentence, the object, or possessive? "Tracie and Alana" are the subjects of the sentence. Next, ask yourself:

Is the noun singular or plural? "Tracie and Alana" is a plural noun. Finally, ask yourself:

Is the noun masculine, feminine, or neuter? "Tracie and Alana" is a feminine plural noun used as the subject of the sentence, so you should replace it with the pronoun "they."

Now the sentences are "Tracie and Alana worked all night. They are tired."

Joelle bought a computer and took the computer to work.

Which pronoun should be used to replace the noun "the computer?" To decide, ask yourself:

Is the noun the subject of the sentence, the object, or possessive? "The computer" is the object of the sentence. Next, ask yourself:

Is the noun singular or plural? "The computer" is a singular noun. Finally, ask yourself:

Is the noun masculine, feminine, or neuter? "The computer" is a neuter singular noun used as the object of the sentence, so you should replace it with the pronoun, "it."

Now the sentence is, "Joelle bought a computer and took it to work."

Choosing between I and me

When you are naming yourself in a sentence with more than one subject, make sure you are using the correct forms of the pronoun for both subjects.

The boss thanked Dave and (I or me?) for our work.

Should you use I, or me, in this sentence? To decide, ask yourself:

Are you the subject or object of the sentence? "The boss" is the subject of the sentence and you are the object, so you should use, "me."

Now the sentence is, "The boss thanked Dave and me for our work."

My sister and (I or me?) went to the mall.

Should you use I, or me, in this sentence? To decide, ask yourself:

Are you the subject or object of the sentence? You and your sister are the subjects of the sentence, so you should use I.

Now the sentence is, "My sister and I went to the mall."
