More than one citation in a piece of writing

If you are including information from different authors or sources, then the citation rules apply to each author or source. The following two paragraphs use information from two different sources.

The first paragraph begins with your own idea: Today's cars are much better mechanically than the cars of earlier generations.

The second sentence begins with a signal phrase with a date introducing a quotation from Browning and ends with the page number in parentheses: As Tara Browning (2016) notes in an article for The Consumer Advocate, "A car that looked great on the outside might have had terrible mechanical or electrical problems" (p.40).

The next sentence is a paraphrase from Browning and includes a citation: In fact, cars of the 1970s and 1980s were often unreliable or even dangerous (Browning, 2016).

The next sentence is your own idea: Mechanical improvements have had some surprising side-effects.

The next sentence begins with a signal phrase with a date, introducing a paraphrase from Upton: According to Arthur Upton (2017), the quality of today's cars has changed the used car market.

The last sentence of the paragraph begins with a second use of a signal phrase for Upton. The date is not required: Upton points out that some exceptionally reliable models are hard to find used because their owners keep them longer.

The citation pattern starts over with a new paragraph. The second paragraph begins with your own ideas: Cars are easier to repair too! In the 1970s, many cars were difficult to work on, and for that reason repairs were expensive.

The third sentence begins with a signal phrase for a quotation from Browning, including a date because this is a new paragraph: According to Tara Browning (2016), "domestic cars and foreign cars were equally bad" (p.40).

The next sentence is your own idea: Today's cars are designed to make it easy for mechanics to fix most problems, which are now diagnosed by hooking the car up to a computer.

Complete paragraphs

Today's cars are much better mechanically than the cars of earlier generations. As Tara Browning (2016) notes in an article for The Consumer Advocate, "A car that looked great on the outside might have had terrible mechanical or electrical problems" (p.40). In fact, cars of the 1970s and 1980s were often unreliable or even dangerous (Browning, 2016). Mechanical improvements have had some surprising side-effects. According to Arthur Upton (2017), the quality of today's cars has changed the used car market. Upton points out that some exceptionally reliable models are hard to find used because their owners keep them longer.

Cars are easier to repair too! In the 1970s, many cars were difficult to work on, and for that reason repairs were expensive. According to Tara Browning (2016), "domestic cars and foreign cars were equally bad" (p.40). Today's cars are designed to make it easy for mechanics to fix most problems, which are now diagnosed by hooking the car up to a computer. In the old days, repairing a car required a lot of guesswork, but computers now help mechanics pinpoint the exact problem quickly and accurately (Browning, 2016). Today's used car will be easier and less expensive to repair than the used cars of previous decades.
