Paraphrasing

When you use all of the ideas and details from a source, but you use your own words to explain what you mean, you are paraphrasing. In a paraphrase, the writer restates all the information from the source using different words and a different sentence structure.

Original source material from A History of the Pony Express by D. Jones, published in 2012:

The Pony Express specifically recruited riders who were small, thus keeping the horses' loads as light as possible.

The main ideas and details:

Pony Express wanted to minimize the weight carried by the horses.

Pony Express hired riders who were as small and light as possible.

Paraphrase:

To reduce the weight carried by the Pony Express horses, the Pony Express management sought slender or undersized riders open parentheses D period Jones comma 2012 close parentheses period.

The paraphrase includes the same main idea as the source: the Pony Express wanted to minimize the weight carried by the horses. It also includes all of the details: the Pony Express hired riders who were as small and light as possible.

The paraphrase is about the same length as the source but uses different wording and sentence structure.

The paraphrase is not enclosed within quotation marks.

The paraphrase includes a citation in parentheses at the end of the last sentence, but before the period.
