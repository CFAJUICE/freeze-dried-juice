Paraphrasing

When you use all of the ideas and details from a source, but you use your own words to explain what you mean, you are paraphrasing. In a paraphrase, the writer restates all the information from the source using different words and a different sentence structure.

Original source material from a book called The Hiker's Guide to Yosemite by P. Martinez, published in 2016:

Those who visit Yosemite National Park are often drawn to its many beautiful waterfalls, including Bridalveil Fall, Vernal Fall, and Ribbon Fall, which is the tallest continuous waterfall in the United States.

The main ideas and details:

People like to visit Yosemite's many beautiful waterfalls.

The details associated with this main idea are: Bridalveil, Vernal, and Ribbon—these are the names of the waterfalls.

Ribbon Fall is the tallest waterfall in the U.S.

Paraphrase:

The waterfalls of Yosemite National Park, including Vernal Fall and Bridalveil Fall, are among the park's most popular attractions. One of Yosemite's waterfalls, the Ribbon Fall, has a place of honor as the waterfall with the largest water drop in the entire country open parentheses Martinez comma 2016 close parentheses period.

The paraphrase includes the same main ideas as the source: People like to visit Yosemite's many beautiful waterfalls. It also includes all of the details: the names of the popular waterfalls—Bridalveil, Vernal, and Ribbon. And that Ribbon Fall is the tallest waterfall in the U.S.

The paraphrase is about the same length as the source but uses different wording and sentence structure.

The paraphrase is not enclosed within quotation marks.

The paraphrase includes a citation in parentheses at the end of the last sentence, but before the period.
