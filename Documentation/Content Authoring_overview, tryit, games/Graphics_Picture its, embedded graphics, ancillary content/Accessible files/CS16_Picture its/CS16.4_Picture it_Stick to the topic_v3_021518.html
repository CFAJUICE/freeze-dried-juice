Stick to the topic

Supporting the main idea

A unified paragraph that sticks to the topic allows readers to focus their attention on the points you are trying to make. When you write a paragraph, make sure that each sentence supports the main idea.

Here is a paragraph about the benefits of riding a bike to work that does not stick to the topic. Which sentences are distracting and do not support the main idea?

Riding a bike to work benefits the biker as well as the environment. If you can commute to work by bicycle, you will fit daily exercise into your busy schedule. Carpooling is another great way to get to work. Additionally, riding a bike can save you considerable money. Unfortunately, riding a bike is not easy in snow or rain. Finally, by cutting back on the amount of miles you drive in your car, you conserve fuel and cut down on carbon dioxide building up in our atmosphere.

Sentences that don't stick to the topic

Two sentences in the paragraph do not support the main idea that riding a bike to work benefits the biker as well as the environment.

"Carpooling is another great way to get to work" takes the reader's focus away from the benefits of biking.

"Unfortunately, riding a bike is not easy in snow or rain" points out a problem with biking.

Each of these sentences weakens the paragraph and should be removed.

Unified paragraph

Here is the paragraph after removing the two sentences that do not stick to the topic. It is much better!

Riding a bike to work benefits the biker as well as the environment. If you can commute to work by bicycle, you will fit daily exercise into your busy schedule. Additionally, riding a bike can save you considerable money. Finally, by cutting back on the amount of miles you drive in your car, you conserve fuel and cut down on carbon dioxide building up in our atmosphere.
