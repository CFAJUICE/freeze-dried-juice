Personal pronouns: Putting it all together

A personal pronoun is a word that replaces a noun in a sentence.

Deciding what pronoun to use

To decide what pronoun to use, match the number, gender, and form of the noun you are replacing. The number can be singular or plural. The gender can be masculine, feminine, or neuter. The form can be subject, object, or possessive.

Singular pronouns

Gender Replacing noun as subject Replacing noun as object Replacing noun as possessive
Masculine he him his
Feminine she her her, hers
Neuter it it its
Neuter I me my, mine
Neuter you you your

Plural pronouns

Gender Replacing noun as subject Replacing noun as object Replacing noun as possessive
Masculine they them their, theirs
Feminine they them their, theirs
Neuter they them their, theirs
Neuter we us our, ours
Neuter you your yours

Examples

Singular feminine subject: Jennifer texts on her phone all day. She even texts at the beach.
Singular neuter object: I parked the car in a huge parking lot. Now I can't find it.
Singular masculine possessive: I forgot John's address. I knew his address yesterday.
Plural mixed gender subject: John and Jennifer walked to the car. They remembered where it was.
Plural masculine possessive: We couldn't find Carl and Ming's keys. We looked everywhere for their keys.

Using I or me in a sentence

Use I for the subject and me for the object. Name yourself second!

Examples

Naming yourself as a subject: She and I went to the movies.
Naming yourself as an object: The boss thanked Jen and me for our work.
Tip: To decide if you have chosen the right pronoun, try leaving the other pronoun out of the sentence and see if it sounds correct.

Replacing singular nouns when you don't know the gender:

Do not use their to replace a singular noun if you don't know the gender. Instead choose one of three other options.
1. Use both "his" and "her:" Anybody could have his or her identity stolen. The employee should submit his or her report each week.
Careful! Do not use "their" because the subject is singular.
2. Use one pronoun, either masculine or feminine: The employee should submit her report each week.
3. Rewrite the sentence to make the subject plural: Employees should submit their reports each week.
