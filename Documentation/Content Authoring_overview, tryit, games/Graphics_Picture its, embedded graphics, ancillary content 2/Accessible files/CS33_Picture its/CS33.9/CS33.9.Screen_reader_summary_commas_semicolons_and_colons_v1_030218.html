Commas, semicolons, and colons: Putting it all together

Commas

There are many ways to use commas.

In sentences with two independent clauses (compound sentences)

Use a comma with a joining word to connect the clauses. Example: Dave didn't feel like cooking dinner comma, so he decided to order a pizza.
If the two clauses in the sentence are short, the comma may be omitted. Example: He called and he placed the order.
Careful! If you use a comma without a joining word, you have created a run-on sentence called a comma splice.

In a list

Use a comma to separate items in a list. Include and before the last item. Example: Dave ordered mushrooms comma, peppers comma, olives comma, and pineapple on his pizza.
Use commas between items in a list of phrases. Example: Dave surfed the internet comma, read some articles comma, and answered emails while he waited.
When an event began in the past and continues into the present, use the verb "have" or "has." Example: I have learned a lesson about leaving on time.

Separating introductory words, phrases, and clauses

Use a comma after an introductory word, phrase, or clause. Examples: Dave comma, what did you order on your pizza?
The last time Dave ordered pizza comma, he got different toppings.
When he calls the pizza place comma, the employees recognize his voice right away.

Setting off descriptive information

Use commas if the added information is non-essential. Dave comma, who had worked hard all day comma, waited anxiously for his pizza.
Do not use commas if the added information is essential. Example: The driver who delivered the pizza arrived early and got a generous tip.

Semicolons

Semicolons are used to join to independent clauses, or to separate a list of items, when the items in the list contain commas.

When joining two independent clauses:

Use a semicolon when clauses are connected in meaning. Example: Dave orders pizza a lot semicolon; he doesn't like to cook.
Use a semicolon when the 2nd clause begins with a transitional word or phrase. Example: Dave has many excuses for not cooking semicolon; for one thing comma, he hates doing dishes. Notice that the transitional phrase is followed by a comma.
When an event began in the past and continues into the present, use the verb "have" or "has." Example: I have learned a lesson about leaving on time.

In a series

Use a semicolon to separate items that already contain commas. Example: He has tried pizza from Chicago comma, Illinois semicolon; New York comma, New York semicolon; and Rome comma, Italy.
Caution! Do not use a semicolon to introduce a quotation. Use a comma instead. Example: My boss told me comma, open quotes "You are really going places closed quotes."

Colons

In independent clauses

Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list of items. Example: Pizzas come in these sizes colon: small, medium, large, and extra-large. Caution! Never introduce a list with a semicolon.
Use a colon to join two clauses when the 2nd adds an explanation. Example: The basic rule for delivering pizza is simple colon: if it's not there in 30 minutes then it's free.
