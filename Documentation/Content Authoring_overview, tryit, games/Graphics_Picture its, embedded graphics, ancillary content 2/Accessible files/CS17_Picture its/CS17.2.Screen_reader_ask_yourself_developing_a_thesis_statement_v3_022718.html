Ask yourself: Developing a thesis statement

A thesis statement lets your reader know your plan for writing. When you're developing a thesis statement, first ask yourself:

What am I writing about?

This will be your topic. Next, ask yourself:

Why am I writing?

This will be your purpose. You may be writing to share information, convince someone of something, or to tell a story. Your purpose for writing will determine what additional information your thesis statement needs.

I am writing to share information.

If you are sharing information, think about the categories you will cover. Include these categories in your thesis statement.

An example is, "A good student organizes her time; asks questions as needed; and learns from mistakes."

I am writing to convince someone of something.

If you are trying to be convincing, think about your opinion, and what reasons best support your opinion. Include your opinion and reasons in your thesis statement.

An example is, "Everyone should read Wuthering Heights because it is an epic love story, a spine-tingling ghost story, and an unforgettable tale of an orphan who has a powerful effect on the family that adopts him."

I am writing to tell a story.

If you are telling a story, think about what hint you can provide without giving away all of the details of the story. This will indicate what you plan to write about to your reader.

An example is, "I would never have imagined that a ten-minute journey across town would take six hours and end with an amazing surprise."
